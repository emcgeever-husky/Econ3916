# The Architecture of Dimensionality: Hedonic Pricing & the FWL Theorem

## Objective

Estimate a multivariate hedonic pricing model on 2026 California real estate data and furnish a first-principles proof of the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell (FWL) Theorem, demonstrating that algorithmic ceteris paribus — the isolation of a single regressor's marginal effect — is not merely a statistical convenience but a mathematically guaranteed property of OLS projection geometry.

---

## Data

**Source:** Zillow Synthetic California Real Estate Dataset, 2026  
**Variables of interest:**

- `Sale_Price` — transaction price of the residential property (USD)
- `Property_Age` — age of the structure at time of sale (years)
- `Distance_to_Tech_Hub` — straight-line distance to the nearest major technology employment center (miles)

---

## Methodology

**Stage 1 — Naive Univariate Benchmark**
- Estimated a simple OLS regression of `Sale_Price` on `Property_Age` alone to establish the unadjusted age coefficient as a baseline for bias quantification.

**Stage 2 — Multivariate OLS Specification**
- Extended the model to include `Distance_to_Tech_Hub` as a control variable, fitting a full hedonic pricing equation via `statsmodels.formula.api`.
- Extracted the adjusted partial coefficient on `Property_Age`, which strips out the shared covariance attributable to location.

**Stage 3 — Manual FWL Proof via Residual Partialling**
- **Step 1 (Partial out the confounder from the outcome):** Regressed `Sale_Price` on `Distance_to_Tech_Hub`; retained residuals representing variation in price *orthogonal* to proximity.
- **Step 2 (Partial out the confounder from the regressor of interest):** Regressed `Property_Age` on `Distance_to_Tech_Hub`; retained residuals representing variation in age *orthogonal* to proximity.
- **Step 3 (Regress residual on residual):** Estimated a simple OLS model of price residuals on age residuals — no intercept required — to recover the isolated marginal effect of age after all shared distance-driven variance has been removed from both series.

**Stack:** Python 3.10+, `pandas`, `statsmodels`, `matplotlib`

---

## Key Findings

**Omitted Variable Bias (OVB) — Magnitude and Direction**  
The naive univariate model produced a materially inflated (in absolute magnitude) coefficient on `Property_Age`. Because newer construction is systematically clustered closer to technology employment centers — and proximity independently commands a price premium — omitting `Distance_to_Tech_Hub` caused the age regressor to absorb location-driven price variation it had no causal claim to. The result was a biased estimate that misattributed economic value generated by *where* a property sits to the incidental fact of *when* it was built.

**FWL Theorem — Exact Numerical Recovery**  
Upon manually partialling out `Distance_to_Tech_Hub` from both `Sale_Price` and `Property_Age` and regressing the resulting residual series against one another, the recovered coefficient matched the multivariate OLS estimate to machine precision. This confirms the core assertion of the FWL Theorem: the partial slope produced by full multivariate OLS is *algebraically identical* to the slope obtained by projecting both the dependent variable and the regressor of interest onto the orthogonal complement of the remaining controls. Ceteris paribus is not an interpretive convention — it is a structural consequence of how OLS partitions variance in a multi-dimensional regressor space.

---

## Takeaway

This lab operationalizes a fundamental insight in applied econometrics: coefficient estimates in multivariate regression are not simply "adjusted" for controls in some heuristic sense. They are geometrically exact projections onto subspaces from which confounding variation has been eliminated. The FWL Theorem makes that geometry explicit, and the manual residual proof makes it empirically verifiable — one of the cleanest bridges between linear algebra and causal inference available to the practicing economist.
